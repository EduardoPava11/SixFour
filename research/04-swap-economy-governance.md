# 04 — Non-Monetary Swap-Economy & Decentralized Governance

*A literature review and design brief for SixFour's grant-based gene exchange and guild governance.*

**Abstract.** SixFour lets users exchange content-addressed "genes" (learned parameter blobs) with no money, where an accepted trade *grants* both parties access without stripping anyone — holdings are monotone and only ever grow. This brief surveys the PhD-level literature on money-free exchange (housing markets, matching, gift/commons economies), reputation and sybil resistance, social choice and preference aggregation, and DAO governance, then argues that the grant (non-rivalrous) semantics fundamentally relaxes the classical mechanism-design impossibilities that plague transfer-based barter, while sharpening the reputation-gaming and sybil problems that the `demand`/`reliability` folds and Dunbar-capped guilds must confront.

## 1. Framing for SixFour

The SixFour swap-economy is defined by three deliberate departures from a classical market:

1. **No money.** There is no numeraire, no transferable token, no price. Exchange is pure barter over indivisible objects (genes).
2. **Grant, not transfer.** An accepted `Trade` event *grants* both proposer and counterparty access to each other's gene. Nobody's holdings shrink. Formally the holdings map is **monotone**: for every agent *i*, `holdings_i(epoch+1) ⊇ holdings_i(epoch)`. Genes are **non-rivalrous** information goods (copyable at zero marginal cost), so "giving" a gene is duplication, not alienation. This is closer to an open-source gift economy than to a barter market.
3. **Governance as pure folds of an append-only ledger.** `Spec.Trade` is a log of `Trade` events (`offer, want, proposer, counter, state ∈ {Proposed, Accepted, Declined, Expired}, epoch`). Reputation scalars are computed deterministically as folds: `demand` = how many of your proposals were taken (a taken-up-ness / reputation measure); `reliability` = accepted / settled ∈ [0,1] (a trust scalar). `Spec.GuildScale` sets Council = 7 deciders (odd → unique median and tie-break; quorum = 4), Guild cap = 150 (Dunbar), with a schism-split when the cap is exceeded.

The central research question is therefore **not** the standard one ("how do we clear a market efficiently and truthfully?"), because a grant model has no scarcity to allocate. It is instead: **when the exchange itself is (nearly) frictionless and non-rivalrous, where does the economy's real scarcity and manipulability move?** The answer, developed below, is that it moves into (a) *attention and reputation* — the `demand`/`reliability` scalars — and (b) *governance legitimacy* — who sits on councils and who is admitted to guilds. Those are exactly the surfaces an adversary will game, and exactly where the classical impossibility results (Gibbard–Satterthwaite, the sybil/plutocracy trade-off) still bite with full force.

## 2. Literature survey by thread

### 2.1 Mechanism design & incentive compatibility without money

The foundational obstacle to any non-dictatorial voting or ordinal mechanism is the **Gibbard–Satterthwaite theorem**: any deterministic, non-dictatorial social choice rule over ≥ 3 alternatives with unrestricted preferences is manipulable (Gibbard 1973; Satterthwaite 1975). Every downstream result in this brief is either an escape route from G–S (restrict the domain, or randomize) or an inheritance of it.

For **exchange without money**, the canonical model is the **Shapley–Scarf housing market** (Shapley & Scarf 1974): each agent is endowed with one indivisible good, has strict preferences over all goods, and no monetary transfers are permitted. David Gale's **Top-Trading-Cycles (TTC)** algorithm computes the unique allocation in the *strong core* when preferences are strict; TTC is **strategyproof** and in fact **group-strategyproof**, and it is characterized as the unique rule satisfying Pareto-efficiency, individual rationality (respecting endowments), and strategyproofness (Roth 1982; Ma 1994). Recent work reconfirms and sharpens this characterization — e.g. via *respecting-improvement* (Barcelona School of Economics working paper, 2024) [1] — and extends it to indifferences and externalities [2]. TTC is the cleanest existing model of *money-free swaps* and is the natural comparison point for SixFour.

For two-sided assignment, the **Gale–Shapley deferred-acceptance (DA)** algorithm produces a stable matching, and the proposing side's version is strategyproof *for the proposers* — but **Roth (1982) proved that no stable matching mechanism is strategyproof for all agents** [3]. This one-sidedness is a structural fact, not an implementation flaw.

When money *is* reintroduced but trade is bilateral and private, the **Myerson–Satterthwaite theorem** (1983) shows that no Bayes–Nash incentive-compatible mechanism can be simultaneously efficient, individually rational, and budget-balanced; **VCG/Groves** mechanisms recover efficiency and truthfulness only by running a deficit (violating budget balance), and the second-best mechanism captures at least ½ of the first-best gains [4]. The lesson for SixFour is inverted but important: *money is what makes these impossibilities bite*. Remove transfers and the budget-balance constraint disappears; remove rivalry (grant semantics) and the core allocation problem itself dissolves (see §3–4).

### 2.2 Gift & commons economies

Because genes are non-rivalrous and given by duplication, the closest economic analogue is not barter but the **gift economy**. Mauss's *The Gift* (1925) established that gifts carry a social obligation to reciprocate (the Maori *hau*, "the spirit of the gift"), binding giver and receiver in an ongoing relationship rather than a cleared transaction [5]. Lewis Hyde extended this to creative and cultural commons, arguing gift exchange is how artistic communities sustain themselves [6]. Empirically, **open-source software** has been analyzed as a gift economy in which *successful gift-giving accumulates reputation for generosity* — the programmer's currency is esteem, not money (Bergquist & Ljungberg 2001; Zeitlyn 2003) [7]. This is a precise template for SixFour: `demand` (how often your genes are taken up) is the reputational return on a gift.

For governing shared resources, **Elinor Ostrom's *Governing the Commons*** (1990) derived **eight design principles** for durable common-pool-resource institutions, from a large empirical study of fisheries, irrigation, and forests [8][9]: (1) clearly defined boundaries (who may withdraw); (2) congruence of rules with local conditions; (3) collective-choice arrangements (those affected can modify the rules); (4) monitoring by accountable monitors; (5) graduated sanctions; (6) low-cost conflict-resolution mechanisms; (7) minimal recognition of the community's right to self-organize by external authorities; and (8) nested enterprises — governance organized in multiple polycentric layers. Wilson, Ostrom & Cox (2013) generalized these into "core design principles" for the efficacy of *any* group [10]. Ostrom's principles map almost one-to-one onto guild governance (§4).

As a money-free medium of exchange, **mutual-credit / LETS** systems (Michael Linton's 1983 Comox Valley LETSystem) are instructive: members exchange goods and services logged in a central, publicly visible ledger, with each member holding an interest-free line of credit; balances net to zero across the community [11][12]. LETS shows that *an append-only, publicly visible ledger with per-member accounts is a workable substrate for a non-monetary economy* — which is exactly `Spec.Trade`. The difference is that LETS credit is rivalrous and can go negative (creating free-rider and default risk), whereas SixFour's grant model is monotone and cannot.

### 2.3 Reputation & trust systems

**EigenTrust** (Kamvar, Schlosser & Garcia-Molina, WWW 2003) computes a global trust value for each peer as the **left principal eigenvector of the normalized local-trust matrix** *C* — i.e. your reputation is the reputation-weighted sum of what others think of you, a PageRank-style fixed point [13][14]. Its crucial sybil-defense mechanism is a set of **pre-trusted peers**: the iteration mixes in a fixed distribution over trusted seeds, which (a) guarantees the Markov chain is aperiodic and strongly connected so the eigenvector exists and is unique, and (b) breaks **malicious collectives** — colluding sybils who rate each other highly gain no global trust because trust must "flow in" from the pre-trusted set. Normalization prevents a peer from assigning arbitrarily high trust to its colluders. This is the canonical answer to "how do you make a reputation scalar collusion- and sybil-resistant": *anchor it in a trusted seed set and make it a flow, not a self-declared count.*

The sybil problem itself — one adversary forging many identities — was named by Douceur (2002) and is provably unstoppable without either a trusted identity authority or a resource cost. **SybilGuard** (Yu, Kaminsky, Gibbons & Flaxman, SIGCOMM 2006) and **SybilLimit** (Yu, Gibbons, Kaminsky & Xiao, IEEE S&P 2008 / ToN 2010) bound the number of accepted sybils using the **social graph**: honest regions are fast-mixing, but the *attack edges* between honest and sybil regions form a sparse cut that random-route intersections can detect. SybilLimit accepts only O(log n) sybils per attack edge — near-optimal, ~200× better than SybilGuard on a million-node graph [15][16]. The key structural insight: **sybils are cheap to create but the trust edges connecting them to honest users are not**, so defenses should key on the graph topology of endorsements, not on identity counts.

The classic failure modes a reputation fold must resist are **whitewashing** (an agent with bad reputation abandons its identity and re-enters fresh) and **feedback manipulation** (self-dealing / collusive ratings). Whitewashing is only deterrable if newcomers start below neutral or must earn standing (Feldman & Chuang 2005) — directly relevant to how `reliability` should treat a zero-history account.

### 2.4 Social choice & preference aggregation

SixFour aggregates a Council of 7 by median/majority judgment. The theoretical backbone is **Moulin's (1980)** characterization: on a **single-peaked** preference domain, a voting rule is strategyproof, anonymous, and efficient **iff** it is a **generalized median** — the median of the agents' peaks together with (n−1) fixed "phantom" ballots [17][18]. The ordinary median voter is the special case. This is one of the few clean *positive* escapes from Gibbard–Satterthwaite, and it is *why a median rule is well-founded*: on a one-dimensional quality axis, the median is the unique strategyproof anonymous efficient aggregator, and **an odd number of voters gives a unique median with no tie to break**.

**Majority Judgment** (Balinski & Laraki, 2007; book 2010/2011) generalizes the median to graded ballots: each judge assigns an ordinal grade, and the winner is the candidate with the **highest median grade**, with a deterministic tie-break that removes median grades one at a time [19][20]. Balinski and Laraki prove highest-median rules are the **most strategy-resistant** deterministic rules — they minimize the fraction of the electorate with an incentive to grade dishonestly — and that MJ escapes Arrow's impossibility by using *grades* (a common language) rather than *rankings* [19][21]. Vannucci (2019) gives a formal strategyproofness characterization of majority judgment. Caveats verified in the literature: like every deterministic non-dictatorial rule with ≥ 3 options, MJ is still manipulable in general (Gibbard's theorem), and it violates the participation criterion (no-show paradoxes) [20]. For a 7-member Council these caveats are mild; the strategy-resistance and unique-median properties dominate.

**Quadratic Voting** (Lalley & Weyl 2015/2018; Weyl 2017) lets agents buy votes at a cost equal to the *square* of the number bought, funded into a rebated pool; it is approximately welfare-efficient as the population grows because the quadratic cost makes marginal vote price proportional to votes held, so agents reveal cardinal intensity truthfully [22][23]. QV is the reference design for *expressing intensity*, but its efficiency proof **requires a transferable budget** (the "voice credits"). SixFour has no money, so QV is a source of ideas (intensity-weighting, convexity to deter concentration) rather than a directly deployable mechanism (see §4).

### 2.5 Preference learning (ties to the value heads)

SixFour already trains value/preference heads with **Bradley–Terry**. The **Bradley–Terry–Luce (BTL)** model gives the probability that item *i* beats *j* as the logistic of their latent-utility difference, `P(i ≻ j) = σ(u_i − u_j)` [24][25]. It is the workhorse of ranking-from-pairwise-comparisons and of **dueling bandits** — the online setting where a learner repeatedly picks a pair and observes noisy preference feedback, minimizing regret against the best arm (Yue et al. 2012; contextual and neural extensions) [26]. The Plackett–Luce generalization handles k-way (subset) comparisons [27]. A 2024 critique — *Rethinking Bradley–Terry Models in Preference-Based Reward Modeling* — cautions that BTL assumes a single global utility and transitive preferences, which can misfit when preferences are cyclic or context-dependent [28]; and 2026 work asks what preference learning actually *recovers* from comparison data [29]. Relevance: the `demand` scalar is itself an implicit tournament (which genes get taken over which), so BTL/dueling-bandit machinery is the principled way to turn the trade ledger into a *value ranking of genes* — and its transitivity assumption is a modeling risk to flag.

### 2.6 DAO / decentralized governance

The most directly relevant recent result is **"Concave is the New Linear: The Impossibility of Anti-Plutocratic DAO Governance"** (Bennett, Vander Vos, Le & Belenkiy, arXiv:2605.18990, 2026) [30]. It proves a formal impossibility: a vote-weighting function **cannot be simultaneously anti-plutocratic and sybil-resistant**. Linear weighting (one token = one vote) resists plutocracy poorly-vs-well trade-offs but is sybil-vulnerable in the identity sense addressed by concavity; concave weighting (diminishing returns, e.g. quadratic) resists sybils' *stake-splitting* only by concentrating power in large holders — i.e. any weighting is on a Pareto frontier between the two harms. This is the DAO-native restatement of the sybil tension in §2.3 and is the sharpest single lens for evaluating SixFour's reputation folds.

Other governance primitives: **Futarchy** (Hanson 2000/2013), "vote on values, bet on beliefs" — democracy sets a welfare metric, prediction markets choose the policy expected to maximize it [31][32]. **Conviction voting** gives a proposal voting weight that *accrues over time* as support persists, favoring sustained over flash preference [32]. The empirical DAO literature repeatedly documents **token plutocracy** — governance power concentrating in large holders, undermining the decentralization premise [30][33]. SixFour has no tradeable token, which structurally sidesteps *stake-based* plutocracy — but §2.3's warning re-enters: reputation itself can concentrate, and a whale of `demand` is a plutocrat by another name.

## 3. Comparative analysis: incentive compatibility under grant (not transfer) semantics

The decisive modeling fact is that SixFour's grant model makes genes **non-rivalrous** and holdings **monotone**. Classical exchange theory assumes rivalry: giving my house to you means I no longer have it, which is *what creates* the allocation problem, the core, and the manipulation incentives. Under grant semantics, "who ends up with the gene" is not a scarce allocation — everyone who touched it keeps it — so several impossibilities simply **do not apply**, while others (reputation, governance) apply undiminished.

| Mechanism / result | Domain assumption | Holds under **transfer** barter | Holds under **grant** (SixFour) | Why the grant model changes it |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Shapley–Scarf **TTC** (strong-core, group-strategyproof) | rivalrous indivisible endowments, strict prefs | ✔ core is the point of the mechanism | **N/A / trivialized** | No rivalry ⇒ no reallocation; the "core" is everyone-gets-everything-they-want-to-copy. TTC solves a problem SixFour doesn't have. |
| **Gale–Shapley** DA / stable matching | two-sided, one seat each | ✔ (proposer-strategyproof only, Roth) | **N/A for gene copy**; relevant only for *scarce seats* (council slots, guild membership) | Genes aren't matched to unique owners; but *guild admission* IS a scarce two-sided match — DA/priority ideas resurface there. |
| **Myerson–Satterthwaite** (no efficient IR-BB bilateral trade) | money, private values, bilateral | ✔ binds hard | **Dissolves** | No money ⇒ no budget-balance constraint ⇒ the impossibility's premise is void. |
| **VCG / Groves** | quasilinear utility, transfers | ✔ efficient but deficit | **N/A** | Requires monetary transfers SixFour forbids. |
| **Gibbard–Satterthwaite** | ≥3 alternatives, ordinal voting | ✔ | **✔ (still binds on all voting/governance)** | Grant semantics don't touch *voting*; councils remain manipulable in principle ⇒ need median/MJ escapes. |
| **Moulin generalized-median** (strategyproof on single-peaked) | 1-D single-peaked | ✔ | **✔ — the recommended escape** | Quality/preference over genes is plausibly single-peaked; median is strategyproof. Odd council ⇒ unique median. |
| **Majority Judgment** (highest-median, strategy-resistant) | graded ballots | ✔ | **✔ — recommended for panel aggregation** | Independent of rivalry; buys strategy-resistance + Arrow-escape via grades. |
| **EigenTrust / SybilLimit** (reputation, sybil defense) | identities + endorsement graph | ✔ | **✔ — MORE important, not less** | Grant model moves scarcity into *reputation/attention*; that's the new attack surface. |
| **"Concave is the New Linear"** (no anti-plutocratic + anti-sybil weighting) | weighted voting | ✔ | **✔ — governs the reputation-weighting choice** | Whatever function turns `demand` into influence faces this frontier. |
| **Quadratic Voting** (intensity-efficient) | transferable voice credits | ✔ | **Partial / adapt only** | Needs a transferable budget SixFour lacks; usable as an *intensity-weighting shape*, not as-is. |

**Bottom line:** the grant model is a genuine *simplification on the allocation side* — the hard money-free-exchange theorems (TTC, Myerson–Satterthwaite, VCG) are neutralized because there is nothing rivalrous to allocate — and a genuine *complication on the reputation and governance side*, where every impossibility (G–S, sybil/plutocracy) survives intact and, if anything, matters more.

## 4. Design implications for SixFour

**(a) The grant model does change the mechanism-design analysis — decisively, and in SixFour's favor.** Because genes are non-rivalrous and holdings are monotone, the exchange layer needs *no* clearing mechanism, *no* core, and *no* strategyproof allocation rule. The right mental model for the swap layer is **gift economy + open-source reputation** (Mauss [5]; Hyde [6]; open-source-as-gift [7]), not barter-market clearing. A `Trade` should never be blocked on efficiency grounds; the only meaningful states are social (Accepted / Declined / Expired). This is why the monotone-grant design is principled: it *removes* the forced-scarcity that produces newcomer lock-out and the manipulability of transfer barter.

**(b) Is TTC or deferred-acceptance relevant when nobody loses their gene? Mostly no — with one exception.** TTC and DA solve *rivalrous* assignment; when copying is free, "who gets the gene" is not contested, so TTC is a solution to a non-problem here (see §3). **The exception is the genuinely scarce goods in the system: Council seats and Guild slots.** A 150-cap guild admitting from a queue, or a 7-seat council filled from candidates, *is* a two-sided matching with capacities — precisely the DA/priority domain. There, deferred-acceptance-style priority rules (rank applicants by `reliability`, admit stably up to the Dunbar cap) are the well-founded choice, and Roth's one-sided-strategyproofness result [3] tells you which side to make the "proposer" (make the *guild's* priority order fixed and public so applicants can't game it, and let applicants propose — applicant-proposing DA is strategyproof for applicants).

**(c) Making `demand`/`reliability` sybil-resistant and non-gameable.** These scalars are currently *self-referential counts* (fold over your own proposals), which is exactly the shape EigenTrust warns against — a colluding ring can inflate each other's `demand` by trading among themselves. Concrete recommendations, each grounded:
- **Make `demand` a flow, not a count (EigenTrust [13][14]).** Weight "your gene was taken by *j*" by *j*'s own standing, computed as a principal-eigenvector fixed point over the take-up graph, and **anchor it in a pre-trusted seed set** (founders, or high-`reliability` veterans). A collusive ring gains nothing because trust must flow in from the seeds; normalization caps how much any one endorser contributes. This is the single highest-leverage change.
- **Key defenses on the endorsement graph, not identity counts (SybilLimit [15][16]).** Sybils are cheap; the *take-up edges* from honest users to the sybil ring are not. Detect anomalous take-up subgraphs (dense internal, sparse external cut) and discount them.
- **Deter whitewashing (Feldman–Chuang [7-adjacent]).** Start zero-history accounts *below* neutral `reliability`, or require earned standing before proposals count toward others' `demand`, so abandoning a bad identity to re-enter fresh is not profitable.
- **Respect the concave/linear impossibility [30].** Whatever function converts `demand`/`reliability` into *governance influence* cannot be both anti-plutocratic and anti-sybil. Choose the point on that frontier explicitly: since SixFour's pre-trusted seed set already provides a sybil floor (per EigenTrust), you can afford a *concave* influence curve (diminishing returns on reputation) to blunt reputation-plutocracy — accepting the residual sybil risk that the seed anchoring already mitigates.

**(d) Why majority-judgment + odd council is well-founded.** Two independent theorems justify the choice. **Moulin (1980) [17][18]:** on a single-peaked quality axis the median is the *unique* strategyproof, anonymous, efficient aggregator — and an **odd** council of 7 yields a **unique median with no tie**, which is precisely why 7 (not 6 or 8) and quorum 4 are the right constants. **Balinski–Laraki [19][20][21]:** graded majority judgment is the *most strategy-resistant* deterministic rule and escapes Arrow by aggregating grades rather than rankings — ideal for a 7-decider panel scoring genes/proposals on a common ordinal scale. Flag the two verified caveats (residual manipulability per Gibbard; no-show paradox), which are negligible at n = 7. Note the pleasing coherence with SixFour's existing Bradley–Terry value training: BTL gives a *cardinal* latent quality, MJ gives a *strategyproof ordinal* aggregation of judges over it.

**(e) Ostrom's principles → guild governance & schism.** The mapping is remarkably direct [8][9][10] and should be treated as a checklist the guild spec must satisfy:
1. **Clear boundaries** → guild membership set is explicit and content-addressed; only members withdraw (copy) from the guild's shared gene pool.
2. **Congruence** → guild rules (reliability threshold, epoch cadence) are local, not globally imposed.
3. **Collective-choice** → members (via the 7-council + MJ) modify their own rules.
4. **Monitoring** → the append-only `Spec.Trade` ledger *is* the monitor; every fold is publicly recomputable, and monitors (councillors) are accountable because their votes are logged.
5. **Graduated sanctions** → declining/expiring, reputation decay, and (ultimately) expulsion should be *graduated*, not binary — a first bad trade shouldn't equal a ban.
6. **Conflict-resolution** → the council is the low-cost dispute forum; make its median ruling cheap and fast.
7. **Right to self-organize** → guilds may set their own admission rules (external authority — the platform — grants minimal recognition, doesn't micromanage).
8. **Nested / polycentric** → **this is exactly the Dunbar-cap schism.** When a guild exceeds 150 it splits, producing nested, polycentric sub-guilds — Ostrom's 8th principle is the theoretical warrant for schism-on-Dunbar-overflow, and it predicts that a *federation* layer (councils of councils) will eventually be needed to coordinate across split guilds.

**(f) Incentive-compatible ways to reward creators (ties to genealogy, [[01-transferable-genes-genealogy]]).** In a gift economy the creator's return is *reputation for generosity* [5][6][7], and `demand` is the natural instrument — but it must reward *lineage*, not just direct takes, or creators of foundational genes that everyone *derives from* go unrewarded. Recommendations:
- **Propagate `demand` along the genealogy DAG.** When gene *g'* (a descendant of *g*) is taken up, award a discounted share of that `demand` up the ancestry — an EigenTrust-style flow over the *provenance graph* rather than the take-up graph. This is incentive-compatible because it rewards being *upstream and useful*, which cannot be faked without actually producing a widely-derived gene, and it aligns with the content-addressed genealogy already in [[01-transferable-genes-genealogy]].
- **Cap the propagation concavely** (per [30]) so a single ancestral "genesis gene" doesn't become a reputation monopoly — diminishing ancestral credit with genealogical distance.
- **Keep it a pure fold.** All of the above are expressible as deterministic folds over the append-only ledger + provenance DAG, preserving SixFour's Haskell-verified, dependency-free ethos: the reputation eigenvector is a fixed-point fold, MJ is a fold over ballots, schism is a fold over membership size.

## 5. Open questions

1. **Eigenvector reputation as a pure fold.** EigenTrust's global trust is an iterative fixed point, not a one-pass fold. Is there a bounded-iteration or closed-form approximation that fits SixFour's "governance = pure fold over an append-only log" contract while preserving sybil-resistance? (Power iteration to a fixed tolerance is a fold over iteration count, but determinism/convergence guarantees need spec-level proof.)
2. **Where to sit on the concave/linear frontier [30]** given SixFour has *reputation* rather than *tokens*. Does anchoring in a pre-trusted seed set genuinely let you take a more anti-plutocratic (concave) influence curve, and can that be formalized as a theorem specific to grant economies?
3. **Non-transitive gene preferences.** BTL/MJ both assume a coherent quality order; if gene "goodness" is genuinely context-dependent (Condorcet-cyclic across users), what aggregation replaces the median without reintroducing Gibbard–Satterthwaite manipulability? [28][29]
4. **Whitewashing vs. newcomer inclusion.** Starting newcomers below neutral deters whitewashing but reintroduces the very newcomer lock-out the grant model was designed to avoid. What is the incentive-compatible sweet spot?
5. **Federation beyond Dunbar.** Ostrom's 8th principle predicts a nested layer above split guilds; what is the strategyproof rule for *inter-guild* council formation, and does Roth's stable-matching impossibility [3] constrain it?
6. **Adapting QV's intensity-expression without money.** Can conviction-voting-style time-accrual [32] substitute for QV's transferable voice credits to let councillors express *intensity* over gene proposals under a no-money constraint?
